Johann Brehmer

Research at the boundary of machine learning and high-energy physics

I'm a Moore-Sloan postdoc at the Center for Data Science and the Center for Cosmology and Particle Physics at New York University, and a part of Kyle Cranmer's group. I grew up a particle physicist, studying effective field theories for the LHC and the phenomenology of the Higgs boson. Now I'm working on methods that can extract more scientific insights from collected data with a mixture of statistics and deep learning.

Research

I am interested in methods that let us infer scientific knowledge from data. My main focus is simulation-based (or likelihood-free) inference: statistical methods for the common case when we can model a phenomenon with a computer simulation, but we cannot calculate its likelihood function. We have pioneered new techniques that combine some understanding of the latent processes in the simulator, a dash of classical statistics, and a heavy dose of machine learning.

In particle physics, these methods allow us to measure fundamental physics properties more precisely than before. I am the lead developer of the MadMiner library, which makes it straightforward to apply these algorithms to almost any LHC problem. I also worked on forecasting methods based on information geometry for particle physics and studied the phenomenology of effective field theories, a language that lets us characterize for instance the properties of the Higgs boson.

We generalized some of the methods that we originally developed for particle physics and applied them to a very different problem: to learn about dark matter through gravitational lensing. In fact, these new ideas are not at all limited to physics, and I am excited to find out how they can help us understand what is happening in scientific fields ranging from economics to epidemiology.

Beyond scientific use cases, I am generally interested in (approximate) inference, normalizing flows, and generative models. Lately I have been developing manifold-modeling flows, a new class of generative models that simultaneously learn the data manifold as well as a tractable probability density on that manifold.

Code

Together with Felix Kling, Irina Espejo, and Kyle Cranmer, I have developed the MadMiner library, which automates machine learning-powered techniques for simulation-based inference for particle physics experiments.

You may also be interested in my GitHub repo, which contains the code used for almost all of my papers, including the code for manifold-modeling flows.
